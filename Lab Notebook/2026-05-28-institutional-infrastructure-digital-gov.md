## Context

Systematic mapping of graduate programs (master's, doctoral) in digital government / e-government in the US and Europe. Goal: assess whether the field operates as an autonomous discipline or as a sub-area of Public Administration (PA) or Information Systems (IS).

## Key Findings

**Disciplinary positioning — four institutional patterns observed:**

1. **Track within PA school** — most common in the US (e.g., MPA/PhD programs at SUNY Albany, Arizona State, George Washington)
2. **Track within IS / informatics school** — dominant in Scandinavian and Central European institutions
3. **Interdisciplinary institute with degree** — rarer; stronger signal of emerging autonomy (e.g., Danube University Krems)
4. **Research center attached to program** — provides field identity without curricular autonomy (e.g., Oxford Internet Institute, Manchester IDPM)

**Tentative periodization:** First dedicated programs appear in the late 1990s–early 2000s. Rename/rebrand pattern is common — programs often reframe predecessors (e.g., "e-government" → "digital governance" → "digital transformation").

**US vs. Europe:** US programs are more likely housed in PA schools and affiliated with NASPAA/ASPA. European programs show stronger IS lineage (IFIP WG 8.5, EGPA), with notable exceptions (Tallinn, Krems) pointing toward partial autonomy.
